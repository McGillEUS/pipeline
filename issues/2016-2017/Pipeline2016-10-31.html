Fun Fact:

Samhain (pronounced sah-win), a Gaelic festival halfway between the winter and autumnal solstices, marks the beginning of winter and the end of harvest. The holiday was celebrated between sunset on October 31 to sunset on November 1, a time in which the doorway to the otherworld was open; souls of the dead/monsters, Fomorians, were believed to cross into our world. It was not uncommon for large fires to be lit in commemoration of the departing sun, or sacrifices of food and even humans to be made to ward off the Fomorian.
The holiday was absorbed into All Saints Day in the beginning of the 8th century by Pope Gregory III, as a time to honor saints and martyrs. The day proceeding All Saints Day was referred to as Hallows' Eve, and later Halloween as we understand it today. Though entirely secularized to date, Halloween carries many variations of both the celtic and christian traditions.
